Effects of Sleep Deprivation.

September 28, 2021 by Donielle Hope

Although the ideal amount of sleep varies from person to person, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommends that individuals obtain at least seven hours of sleep per night. They also believe that one out of every three adults does not get enough sleep.Interruptions to sleep can be annoying, but a consistent lack of quality sleep can have a negative impact on a person's performance at work or school, capacity to function day to day, quality of life, and health.

A person who does not receive enough good sleep may experience a variety of symptoms, including: fatigue, irritability, mood changes, difficulty focusing and remembering, and a reduced sex drive. Sleep deprivation can affect various aspects of health. The immune system: Lack of sleep might make a person more susceptible to infections that take longer to heal and respiratory disorders. Weight: The hormones that control hunger and fullness can be affected by sleep. It can also cause insulin to be released. Sleep disruptions can lead to increased fat accumulation, weight gain, and an increased risk of type 2 diabetes. The cardiovascular system: Sleep aids in the healing and rebuilding of heart vessels, as well as the procedures that keep blood pressure, sugar levels, and inflammation under control. Sleep deprivation has been linked to an increased risk of cardiovascular disease. Hormone levels: Lack of sleep can impact hormone production, particularly growth hormone and testosterone production. It also causes the release of more stress hormones like norepinephrine and cortisol in the body. The Brain: The prefrontal cortex, which deals with cognition, and the amygdala, which deals with emotion, are both affected by sleep deprivation. Sleep deprivation can also make it difficult to generate new memories, which can hinder learning. Fertility: Sleep deprivation has been linked to a reduction in the production of hormones that promote conception.

Treatments for Sleep Deprivation

September 28, 2021 by Donielle Hope

Counseling, lifestyle and environmental changes, drugs, and alternative therapies are all options for improving sleep quality. A person may also require therapy for an underlying medical problem. Some approaches that do not involve drugs include: Relaxation techniques: Meditation, mindfulness training, breathing exercises, and guided imagery can help reduce tension. Audio recordings and sleep apps can also help. Cognitive behavioral therapy: Known as CBT, this may help a person identify thought patterns that are contributing to limited sleep.

Changing sleeping habits and the sleep environment can often help. A person can: Try going to bed and waking up at the same times every day, even on the weekends, with the goal of establishing a routine. Avoiding eating 2â€“3 hours before bedtime. After trying to fall asleep for 20 minutes, get up and read, then try again later. Get regular exercise during the day. Keep the bedroom quiet, dark, and cool. Turn off electronic devices and keep them away from the sleeping area. Limit the consumption of caffeine and alcohol, especially close to bedtime. Avoid tobacco use. Use a mouth guard to manage bruxism. If these measures do not help, a person should see a healthcare provider, especially if getting too little sleep is affecting the quality of life. Some people find that devices help, including mouth guards, white noise machines, anti-snore devices, sleep trackers, wedge pillows, and other products. These are available for purchase online. However, there is no guarantee that any of these will work.
